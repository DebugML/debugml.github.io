> Large language models (LLMs) often make reasoning errors.
> We introduce a framework that can reliably certify errors with probabilistic guarantees, basing its decision solely on previously-verified premises.
> We find that our framework can reliably detect propagated errors in long reasoning chains, which other methods fail to.


Picture this: you're using an AI to solve a math problem. It starts perfectly—correctly setting up variables and equations. Then, halfway through, it makes a single copying error: changing "2/5" to "3/5." From that point forward, every step follows logically from the previous ones, but the entire chain of reasoning is now fundamentally broken. The final answer is wrong, and worse yet, the AI's confident, step-by-step presentation makes the error nearly impossible to spot.

This scenario isn't hypothetical—it's a critical vulnerability in how large language models (LLMs) handle complex reasoning. When errors propagate through reasoning chains, they can undermine trust in AI systems for high-stakes applications like medical diagnosis, legal analysis, or financial planning.


## The Core Problem: When Logic Becomes a Liability

To understand why current error detection methods fail, let's examine a concrete example:

**Context:**  
The denominator of a fraction is 7 less than 3 times the numerator.  
If the fraction is equivalent to 2/5, what is the numerator?

**Correct Chain:**

1. Let the numerator be x  
2. The denominator is 3x - 7  
3. We know that x/(3x-7) = 2/5  
4. Therefore, 5x = 6x - 14  
5. Finally, we get x = 14 ✓

**Error-Prone Chain:**

1. Let the numerator be x  
2. The denominator is 3x - 7  
3. We know that x/(3x-7) = 3/5 ← <span style='color:orange'>*Ungrounded error*</span>
4. Therefore, 5x = 9x - 20 ← <span style='color:red'>*Invalid derivation*</span>
5. Finally, we get x = 5 ← <span style='color:#880088'>*Propagated error*</span>

The problem isn't just individual mistakes—it's how they cascade. Step 3 contains an <span style='color:orange; font-weight:bold'>**ungrounded error**</span> (incorrect information from context). Step 4 shows an <span style='color:red; font-weight:bold'>**invalid derivation**</span> (mathematical miscalculation). Step 5 demonstrates <span style='color:#880088; font-weight:bold'>**error propagation**</span>: while the logic is internally consistent with step 4, it's wrong because of the earlier error.

Current detection methods struggle because they evaluate entire chains simultaneously, getting confused by these propagated errors. An LLM judge might incorrectly mark step 5 as sound because it logically follows from step 4, missing that step 4 itself was based on faulty premises.


## Detecting Errors via Autoregressive Reasoning Entailment Stability (ARES)

To address this issue, we introduce Autoregressive Reasoning Entailment Stability (ARES), a probabilistic framework inspired by human reasoning, which assesses each reasoning step inductively.

The key insight behind **ARES** is deceptively simple: evaluate each claim based only on previously-verified sound premises—just as humans naturally discount dubious statements when reasoning.

Here's how ARES works:

1. **Map the reasoning landscape:** For each step, consider all possible combinations of which previous claims might be sound or unsound.  
2. **Probabilistic evaluation:** Instead of binary right/wrong judgments, assign each claim a probability of being entailed by sound premises.  
3. **Autoregressive filtering:** Use only claims already deemed sound as premises for evaluating subsequent steps.  
4. **Statistical certification:** Provide rigorous guarantees about the reliability of the entire process.


ARES computes an **entailment stability score** $$\tau_k$$ for each reasoning step $$k$$:


<!-- $$\tau_k = \sum_{\alpha \in \{0, 1\}^{n+k-1}} \mathcal{E}(C(\alpha), C_{n+k}) \cdot \text{Pr}[\alpha]$$ -->

<!-- where $\mathcal{E}$ is the entailment model, $\alpha$ is a binary vector representing the inclusion and exclusion of previous claims, $n$ is the number of base claims, $C(α)$ represents different combinations of sound premises, and $Pr[α]$ is the probability of each combination occurring. -->

$$\tau_{k} = \sum_{\alpha \in \{0,1\}^{n+k-1}} \mathcal{E}(C (\alpha), C_{n+k}) \cdot \Pr[\alpha]$$

where the binary vector $\alpha \in \{0, 1\}^k$ indicates which claims to include ($\alpha_i = 1$) or exclude.

The probability of a premise combination, $\Pr[\alpha]$, is calculated autoregressively.
- For **base claims**, it is the product of their prior soundness probabilities $p_i$: 
$$\Pr[\alpha_{1:n}] = \prod_{i = 1}^{n} p_i ^{\alpha_i} (1 - p_i) ^{\alpha_i}$$
- For **derived claims**, the probability is updated inductively via the chain rule, conditioned on the entailment of the new claim:
$$\Pr[\alpha_{1:n+k}] = \Pr[\alpha_{1:n+k-1}] \,\cdot \quad\, \mathcal{E}(C (\alpha_{1:n+k-1}), C_{n+k})$$


As direct computation is intractable, we estimate $\tau_k$ via efficient Monte Carlo sampling, which comes with a formal statistical guarantee on its accuracy in certifying ARES.

**Theorem 1.** (Ceritifying ARES via Sampling)
Let $N \geq \tfrac{\log(2m/\delta)}{2 \varepsilon^2}$ for any $\varepsilon > 0$ and $\delta > 0$.
Given an entailment model $\mathcal{E}$ and a reasoning chain with $m$ derived claims,
use $N$ i.i.d. samples to estimate each $\tau_k$.
Then, with probability at least $1 - \delta$, we have $|\hat{\tau}_k - \tau_k| \leq \varepsilon$ for all $k$.
{: .notice--info}

The sampling process is illustrated below:

    Then, for each derived claim, we compute its entailment rate with respect to the premise set.
    Finally, we add this derived claim to the premise set with probability equal to its entailment rate.

We attribute this success to ARES being the only method that satisfies all key desiderata for error detection: 
- **_Robust:_** previous errors do not adversely affect the current step.
- **_Causal:_** downstream steps do not affect the current step.
- **_Sufficient:_** all relevant claims are included as premise when assessing.

## Conclusion
In this blog post, we show that ARES offers a novel, certifiable, and probabilistic approach to inductively assess reasoning soundness. By considering only previously validated claims, it provides a more principled and reliable method for detecting errors in reasoning chains.

For more details in thoeretical proofs and quantitative experiments, see our [paper](https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.12948) and [code](https://github.com/fallcat/ares).
